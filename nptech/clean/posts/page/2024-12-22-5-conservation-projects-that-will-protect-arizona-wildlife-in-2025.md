#### 1. Road Rewilding

Arizona is removing old roads and turning them back into natural habitat. Teams take out pavement, plant native plants, and let nature reclaim these areas. This creates wildlife corridors that help bighorn sheep and desert tortoises move safely between areas. The Coronado National Forest stopped using over 160 miles of roads in 2019, and [volunteers are transforming these unused into thriving wildlife paths](https://skyislandalliance.org/2023/10/a-first-experience-with-road-rewilding-surveys/).

#### 2. Border Wildlife Protection

Conservation groups from the U.S. and Mexico are working together to protect wildlife corridors along the border. The Sky Islands region crosses both countries and hosts diverse wildlife. Scientists placed 36 cameras along the border to study how jaguars, mountain lions, and bears move across the area. [Conservationists are partnering with agencies to build wildlife passages](https://skyislandalliance.org/our-work/conservation/border-restoration/) and keep animal pathways open.

#### 3. Solar-Powered Water Stations

Solar-powered water stations are changing wildlife conservation across Arizona’s deserts. These stations provide reliable water in remote areas, helping mountain lions and migrating birds survive hot, dry conditions. The stations collect valuable data about animal behavior and movement. As droughts increase due to climate change, these water stations are lifelines for desert animals.

#### 4. **Pollinator Gardens**

[Communities across Arizona are creating pollinator gardens](https://www.goodfoodfinderaz.com/blog/2024/10/3/how-your-backyard-can-help-preserve-native-plants-amp-arizona-wildlife) in yards, schools, and public spaces. These gardens use native plants that provide food and shelter for bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds. The gardens help endangered species like monarch butterflies recover while inspiring wildlife-friendly gardening. This movement proves conservation can happen in your backyard.

[![Desert pollinator garden featuring winding gravel path, flowering native plants, saguaro cacti, and desert trees with drought-resistant landscaping.](https://i0.wp.com/www.nptechforgood.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/DanielleCarlockCowanYard20191.jpg?resize=960%2C319&ssl=1)](https://www.goodfoodfinderaz.com/blog/2024/10/3/how-your-backyard-can-help-preserve-native-plants-amp-arizona-wildlife) **Photo by [Danielle Carlock Cowan and the Maricopa Native Seed Library](https://www.goodfoodfinderaz.com/blog/2024/10/3/how-your-backyard-can-help-preserve-native-plants-amp-arizona-wildlife)**

#### 5. Partnerships with Native American Tribes

Arizona’s conservation work strengthens through partnerships with Native American tribes. These collaborations combine traditional knowledge with modern wildlife science. The approach works well for protecting golden eagles and managing desert grasslands. Tribal lands have the best remaining habitats, and tribal communities have successfully cared for landscapes for generations.
